The north star: robot farmers

The long game: kids design their own robots in Blender and put them to work in a shared virtual world — a farm — where robots grow food. The world is a simulation testbed: designs are tested against each other in healthy competition, refined season over season, and the best of them graduate toward physically buildable robots. Everything on this page is a step on that path:

  • The quests teach exactly the modeling skills a robot body needs — the bike assembly is already wheels, frames, and fasteners. Quest content should march toward chassis, grippers, and tool mounts.
  • The ghost model becomes the design gate: match the reference chassis to prove your build is sound, then diverge — your robot, your look, your trade-offs.
  • The mascot foreshadows the product. The yellow robot that guides kids today is the same rig category they'll eventually design themselves. “Build a friend like me” is the quest line.
  • The farm keeps competition healthy. Robots compete on yield, energy efficiency, and reliability over a growing season — not combat. Class-level co-op (a shared field) plus per-design leaderboards; constraints (a parts budget, a power budget) make clever design beat brute force, which is exactly the engineering habit that transfers to real robots.
  • Sim-to-real is the graduation: designs carry a bill of materials; a design that wins in the sim exports its printable/buildable parts. (The original desktop player already spoke serial to physical robot bases — that heritage comes back here.)
  • The garden is the endgame: graduated robots get deployed for real in an indoor botanical garden, remotely operated. A kid logs in each day to check on their robot — live camera of its plot, telemetry (battery, tasks done, soil moisture), a time-lapse of what it's growing — and tweaks its behavior for tomorrow. Plants grow on plant time, which makes this the deepest retention loop imaginable: a living thing depends on your engineering.

Making the garden feasible: the workable pattern is a fleet of standardized base chassis (safe, maintained on-site) that run each student's design layer — their attachments, parameters, and block programs — rather than one-off hardware per kid. Remote ops means a queued job model, not live joysticks: the student's daily tweaks are reviewed constraints (watering schedule, route, tool choice) executed within a staff-stewarded safety envelope. Telemetry uses the same schema as the simulation, so the sim is a true digital twin — test tonight's tweak in the sim, then promote it to the real robot. Cameras per plot + nightly time-lapse renders give every login a payoff even when nothing needs fixing.

Technically this stays on the same stack longer than you'd think: Blender exports glTF; a three.js farm world runs in the browser off the same Cloudflare platform; robot behavior starts as simple block programs (sense → move → act) before anyone writes code. Determinism-first physics keeps season results fair and replayable.

The core idea, named

Each “game” is a quest: a guided walkthrough of modeling something real in Blender. The current click-through player is the first mode of a progression that ends with the student doing it unassisted in real Blender:

ModeWhereWhat the student doesWhat verifies it
1 · MimicWeb playerClicks and key-presses on screenshots (today’s game)The player itself
2 · GuidedReal BlenderSame steps in Blender, with assist layers the student toggles (below)Addon checkpoints
3 · Free buildReal BlenderBuilds from the brief aloneFinal-mesh check

Inside Guided mode, assistance is layers, not levels — each one a toggle, so training wheels come off one at a time and the student chooses their own challenge:

  • Highlights — the addon points at the UI region/button for the current step
  • Ghost — the translucent target model in the viewport, on or off at any time
  • Narration — spoken step instructions

Scoring honors the layers: finishing with fewer layers on earns more stars. Mastery is re-doing the same bike with everything off — honest pedagogy and free content.

webAccounts & progress

Stays on Cloudflare, no servers: the existing Worker gains an API and a D1 (SQLite) database. Tables are small: students, quests, step_events (quest, step, timestamp, tries), awards. Progress writes are a beacon per completed step, so resume-where-you-left-off falls out for free.

  • Kid-safe auth: a teacher/parent creates a class; students join with a class code + picture password (pick 3 icons in order). No student email, no PII beyond a display name — keeps you clear of COPPA headaches. Adults get magic-link email.
  • Sessions: signed cookie from the Worker; the Blender addon uses the same token so both surfaces write to one progress record.
  • The start screen becomes a quest map: cards per quest with per-mode stars and assist-layer badges, the robot walking to the student’s current position on the map.

webGamification that teaches, not decorates

  • Skill badges tied to real operators: the hotspot data already knows what each step teaches. Badge names come from Blender’s own vocabulary — Extrude Apprentice, Modifier Wrangler, Loop-Cut Surgeon — earned across quests, not per quest.
  • Stars per level: 3 for no misses, 2 with a few, 1 for completion. Add a per-step miss counter and this is a one-day change.
  • The robot is the reward: the mascot rig has 14 animations (we ship 4). Unlock Jump, Yes/No, Punch… and cosmetics rendered from the same rig — hats, colors. Kids grind for their robot, not for a number. (Bonus: the No clip is a perfect gentle wrong-answer shake, and idle→point after 10 s stuck is a built-in hint system.)
  • Streaks & class view: a teacher dashboard heatmap (students × quests) — D1 query, one page. Leaderboards stay inside a class, opt-in, to keep it kind.
  • Certificates: a printable per-quest diploma with the robot and the model the student built. Cheap, and kids/parents love paper.

addonThe Blender companion

A bpy addon with three jobs, in order of build difficulty:

1 · Guide (Guided mode)

The addon fetches the same quest manifest and walks the student step-by-step inside Blender: viewport overlay text for the instruction (same TTS narration via the web session), and a draw-handler highlight on the region/button the step targets. Sync state through the platform API — no local wiring between browser and Blender needed.

2 · Verify (the “that would be cool” part)

After each step the addon checks the scene, not the click: active object and mode, selection counts, modifier stack, transform values, and the operator history (bpy.context.window_manager.operators) to confirm the student used the taught tool rather than an accidental path. Each passed checkpoint posts a signed event (HMAC with the session token) so progress can’t be spoofed by curl. Verification is per-step cheap and free-build capable: the same checks, run once against the finished file.

3 · Record (the multiplier)

The biggest bottleneck in this platform is authoring content. Flip the addon into recorder mode while an instructor models something: it captures a screenshot, the mouse/keyboard event, region coordinates, and the operator that ran, for every step — which is exactly the frames + hotspots.json + narration-text format the player consumes. An hour of instructor modeling becomes a quest with near-zero editing, and the operator log doubles as the Guided-mode checkpoint script. This one feature turns MSMD from a game into a content pipeline.

addonThe ghost model

Your instinct is right, and it’s very buildable:

  • The quest ships a target mesh. The addon imports it as a non-selectable, x-ray “ghost” in the student’s scene — same location, translucent wireframe.
  • Verification is geometric similarity: object/vert/face counts, bounding-box fit, then a sampled surface-distance score (chamfer distance is a few lines of mathutils.bvhtree).
  • The gamification hook: the ghost solidifies as you match it — sections snap from wireframe to shaded as their distance score passes threshold. The progress bar lives in the 3D viewport itself, which is the most Blender-native reward possible.

From the vision boards

Themes from the “A Path” and UNWASTE FARMS reference boards, translated into platform features:

  • Publish everything. The boards attach “video/publish your progress” to every single step. Build it in: every quest completion offers a one-click share render — the student's stats, their robot, a replay of their build — sized for YouTube/shorts. The recorder pipeline that captures instructor content can capture student runs too. Publishing is the portfolio, and the boards treat it as the habit that compounds.
  • Teams, not just individuals. “Build a team of friends to conquer level 63” — quests should support team runs (relay steps, shared stars) and the farm's class co-op fields are the same idea grown up.
  • Mentors at one hour a week. The UNWASTE board asks every adult for 1 hr/week. Give that a product surface: a mentor account type attached to a class — sees the heatmap, leaves voice-note encouragement (same TTS/audio plumbing), reviews garden job queues. Low commitment, real leverage.
  • The unwaste loop is the sim's game design. Black soldier flies turn food waste into feed, fish feed on the flies, water feeds the beets, beets + fish waste become fertilizer. That closed loop is the farm simulation's actual systems diagram — robots tend each stage, and “zero waste” is the score. The sim teaches the real regenerative cycle the boards want built.
  • Seasonal spectacle. Winter wonderland light gardens from the boards → seasonal events in the sim and, eventually, the real garden: robots hang the lights, and December logins get something worth seeing.
